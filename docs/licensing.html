Decision pending   Last updated 2026-06-21  ·  Owner: Nick / Enspyr Pty Ltd

Where we are. No licence has been committed. There is no LICENSE file at the repo root, no license: field in pubspec.yaml, and the README's "See repository root" points at a file that does not exist. This document records the options, the constraints, and a recommendation.

Direction chosen. Split the licence by layer: a permissive licence on the reusable framework (to seed adoption, the way Andy G uses Apache), and a protective or closed licence on the game itself.

Key finding. The Screen Australia Games Production Fund is a non-recoupable grant that takes no IP stake — it imposes no restriction on how the output is licensed or open-sourced. The only binding instrument still to read is the signed Project Grant Agreement (PGA).

2. Apache 2.0 vs AGPL v3

Your read on these is correct. The short version:

Apache 2.0AGPL v3
TypePermissiveStrong copyleft
Must share changes?No. A business can modify it, ship it inside a proprietary product, and contribute nothing back.Yes — and uniquely, the network clause (§13) closes the "SaaS loophole": anyone running a modified version as a hosted service must offer users the source.
AdoptionBroadest. Asks nothing of downstream users.Narrower. Many companies (Google notably) ban AGPL dependencies outright.
PatentsExplicit patent grant + retaliation clause.Patent grant via GPLv3 terms.
Best forCode you want maximally adopted; goodwill and ecosystem.Code where you want improvements to flow back, or where copyleft is the thing that makes a paid commercial licence worth buying.
The dual-licensing lever (why sole ownership matters)

Because Enspyr Pty Ltd is the sole copyright holder (confirmed in chain-of-title-declaration.md — sole developer, no external IP contributors, no encumbrances), you are not limited to one licence. The classic single-rights-holder move is to release under AGPL and sell a commercial licence to businesses that don't want the copyleft. AGPL becomes the lever: anyone who won't share their changes has to buy their way out.

A pure-Apache release forgoes this entirely — with a permissive licence there is nothing to buy. That's the trade Andy G accepts in exchange for maximum adoption. The split recommended here keeps both: adoption on the framework, the commercial lever on the game.

Note: all current third-party dependencies are permissive (Flutter BSD-3, Flame MIT, LiveKit Apache 2.0, Firebase Apache/BSD), so nothing forces a copyleft licence. AGPL on the game would be a deliberate choice, not an inherited obligation.

3. Screen Australia grant — IP findings

Researched against the primary sources (the Guidelines PDF and Terms of Trade, not the summary web pages). The grant is materially friendlier to open-sourcing than a film/TV investment would be.

QuestionAnswer
Does Screen Australia take any IP?No — no ownership, no equity stake.
Recoupment / revenue share?None — pure grant, not repayable.
Restriction on open-sourcing the output?None stated in guidelines or terms of trade.
Binding instrument still to read?The PGA — the signed Project Grant Agreement defines the actual terms and is not public.

Exact wording

"Funding is provided in the form of a grant. Screen Australia does not expect to recoup funds from successful teams. That is, you are not expected to repay the grant in any way."
— Games Production Fund Guidelines, §6.2 Terms of Funding

The only IP rules in the guidelines concern inbound IP, not how you license your output:

Ineligible: projects that "are based on a licence to use and/or adapt pre-existing intellectual property owned by a third party… For clarity, this does not include licences obtained for music, assets or software/plugins used in the making of the game."
— Guidelines, §3.2.2 Ineligible Projects

Tech World is original work using permissively-licensed tooling, so it clears this cleanly (per the chain-of-title declaration).

Eligibility landmine for the framework/game split — read this

The guidelines list as ineligible:

"business-to-business products (for example, training simulations, games created solely for teaching purposes at schools, games with ties to academic research, middleware tools, or games limited to a small number of locations)"

Implication: open-sourcing the reusable framework as a byproduct of building the game is fine. But the funded thing must be the game. Do not pitch or frame the project as an engine, middleware, or B2B teaching tool — that risks ineligibility. The split licence is safe; the split narrative needs care.

Other grant obligations worth noting
  • 90% Australian spend. At least 90% of the grant must be spent on development expenditure physically in Australia.
  • Credit obligation. Funded projects must include the Screen Australia logo per the Credits Policy and the PGA.
  • Up to $100,000 per application; one application per round across this and the Emerging Gamemakers Fund.

5. Open questions

  • Read the PGA before finalising. It is the binding instrument and may contain IP/distribution clauses not in the public guidelines. (Only relevant if/when the grant is awarded.)
  • Game licence: AGPL vs all-rights-reserved? AGPL = visible + dual-licensing lever; closed = simplest, keeps every option open. Reversible later for code not yet published.
  • Where does the framework/game boundary fall in the repo? The split licence needs a concrete directory boundary (which packages are "framework"). Worth defining before writing the LICENSE files.
  • Contributor licensing. If external contributors ever join, a CLA is needed to preserve the dual-licensing lever. Not needed while Nick is sole developer.
